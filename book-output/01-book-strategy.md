# Phase 2 — Book Strategy

## Title and subtitle

**Title:** *How to Make Money During the AI Boom*
**Subtitle:** *A Capital Allocator's Playbook for Income, Investing, and Wealth Creation in the Age of Intelligent Machines*

## One-page book promise

Most readers will encounter the AI boom the way most people encountered the internet boom of the late 1990s — late, emotionally, and through assets that are already priced for perfection. This book is written so that does not happen to you.

It treats AI not as a software trend but as a once-in-a-generation **capital re-pricing event**: a structural shift in the cost of compute, labor, and distribution that creates a fifteen-to-twenty-year window of asymmetric opportunity. Inside that window, ordinary people, professionals, founders, and investors will quietly become wealthy — not because they predicted the future, but because they understood the **shape** of the value chain, positioned themselves where money actually accrues, and survived the inevitable bubbles and shake-outs along the way.

You will finish this book with:

- A clear mental map of where AI money is being made — and where it only *looks* like it is being made.
- A framework for building AI-leveraged income inside your current job, your business, or a new one.
- A disciplined approach to investing in the AI theme — public markets, private opportunities, and the traps that destroy capital.
- A taxonomy of scams, bubbles, and bad bets to avoid.
- A 12-month execution plan you can start running this week.
- A 10-year positioning strategy that survives whichever specific companies and technologies win.

The book is direct, unsentimental, and written from an allocator's perspective. It does not motivate; it instructs. It does not hype; it positions. It does not predict winners; it teaches you how to think like the people who fund them.

## Target reader profile

Ambitious adults who already work for a living and want to compound capital, skill, and optionality through the AI cycle. Specifically:

- **Professionals and executives** who want to upgrade their earning power and protect their career.
- **Founders and operators** building or scaling businesses where AI is now a strategic input.
- **Consultants and service providers** turning AI into productized cash flow.
- **Investors** — public markets, angel, fund LPs — looking for analytical discipline.
- **Creators** with audiences and the leverage to convert attention into product revenue.
- **Capital allocators** managing personal wealth or family-office portfolios who want a structured view of the cycle.

The book assumes the reader is intelligent, time-pressed, and slightly skeptical. It does not assume a finance background. It does not pander.

## Reader transformation

**Before:** sees AI as a chaotic mix of news headlines, hot stocks, scary predictions, and viral demos. Doesn't know whether to invest, retrain, hire, fire, build, sell, or wait. Suspects the boom is real, fears being left behind, also fears being scammed.

**After:** has a clear value-chain map, a personal positioning thesis, an execution plan for the next 12 months, and an investing framework that survives the next bear market. Can distinguish between durable opportunity and narrative-driven hype. Knows where their personal edge is and how to compound it.

## Core thesis

The AI boom is a **capital re-pricing event** that is restructuring the cost of compute, labor, and distribution simultaneously. Wealth in this cycle accrues to those who:

1. Position **upstream** in the value chain wherever possible (infrastructure, distribution, data, workflow ownership).
2. Build **AI-leveraged income** that compounds with the cycle, not against it.
3. Invest with **price discipline**, not narrative enthusiasm.
4. Avoid the **predictable failure modes** of every prior infrastructure boom.
5. Operate on a **decade-long horizon**, not a quarterly one.

You do not need to predict the winners. You need to be standing in the right place when the winners emerge.

## Contrarian angles

The book takes positions that distinguish it from the average AI book on the shelf:

1. **The application layer is not where most readers should expect to make their money.** Infrastructure, distribution, and data tend to capture more durable value, especially early in the cycle.
2. **AI does not destroy jobs uniformly.** It restructures them. The people who rise are those who absorb AI into their workflow before their employer requires it.
3. **The biggest individual opportunity is not building a startup.** For most readers it is wage leverage and consulting cash flow inside their existing domain expertise.
4. **"AI-native" companies are not automatically better investments than incumbents.** Distribution, customer relationships, and proprietary data often beat raw model capability.
5. **Every infrastructure boom in modern economic history has produced a major bubble and a major bust.** Plan for it. Do not be surprised by it.
6. **Most people will lose money trying to time the cycle.** Position, accumulate, hold; do not trade.
7. **The AI agency / coaching / "side-hustle" economy is largely a transfer of money from aspirants to course sellers.** It is not where serious wealth gets built.
8. **Capital allocators do not chase narratives. They underwrite cash flow, defensibility, and price.** This is true in AI as it has been true in every prior cycle.

## Editorial style guide

**Voice:** A senior capital allocator briefing a small group of trusted operators. Confident because the analysis is strong. Direct because the reader's time is valuable. Unsentimental because markets are.

**Cadence:** Short paragraphs. Strong topic sentences. Concrete nouns. Verbs that move. Long sentences only when the structure of the idea demands it.

**Banned moves:**

- Generic AI-blogger filler ("In today's fast-paced world…").
- Empty intensifiers ("incredible," "amazing," "game-changing").
- Hedging tics ("you might want to consider possibly…").
- False precision ("AI will create $13.7 trillion of value by…") unless verifiably sourced.
- Fabricated quotes, statistics, or case studies.
- Self-promotion or guru posture.
- Cliffhanger marketing copy disguised as chapters.

**Required moves:**

- Lead each chapter with a hook that earns the reader's attention.
- State the thesis explicitly within the first 300 words.
- Use frameworks (named, numbered, repeatable).
- Use illustrative scenarios — clearly marked as illustrative — rather than fabricated case studies.
- End each chapter with **Billionaire Operator Notes**, a chapter summary, and an action checklist.
- Treat the reader as an adult.

## Terminology rules

- **"AI"** in this book means the current wave of large generative and agentic models and the surrounding infrastructure. The book is precise when narrower terms (LLM, foundation model, agent, RAG, fine-tuning) are needed.
- **"The boom"** refers to the current capital and adoption cycle, not a guarantee of indefinite price appreciation.
- **"Capital allocator"** means anyone consciously deploying capital — personal, business, or institutional — toward a return.
- **"Edge"** means a structural reason to expect above-average returns: information, skill, access, time horizon, or behavior.
- **"Moat"** is used in its standard sense (Buffett-era): structural defensibility of cash flows.
- **"Productized service"** means a service offering with a fixed scope, fixed price, and standardized delivery — typically AI-augmented.
- **"Wage leverage"** means using AI tools to increase the value of labor inside an existing role, captured by the worker through advancement, compensation, or transition.
- The book avoids jargon where plain language works. When jargon is unavoidable, it is defined the first time it appears.

## Editorial QA bar (applied chapter-by-chapter)

Every chapter is reviewed against:

1. **Factual discipline** — no invented numbers, quotes, cases, or sources.
2. **Voice** — allocator tone, no drift to motivational-blogger register.
3. **Density** — every paragraph carries weight; no filler.
4. **Frameworks** — at least one named, repeatable framework per chapter.
5. **Action** — the reader can execute something from each chapter this week.
6. **Risk** — every chapter explicitly addresses how the reader can be wrong or harmed.
7. **Standalone value** — a reader who picks up the book at any chapter gets something they can use immediately.
8. **Disclaimer compliance** — no statements that cross into personalized financial advice.
