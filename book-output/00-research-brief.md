# Phase 1 — Research Brief & Fact Map

**Project:** *How to Make Money During the AI Boom*
**Scope:** Pre-draft research grounding for a 40,000-word commercial non-fiction book.
**Compiled:** 2026-05-08

---

## 0. Operating constraint (read first)

This brief is written without live internet access. The author cannot pull fresh price quotes, quarterly earnings, regulatory rulings, or current market caps. Every claim below is therefore tagged:

- **[V] Verified** — established and stable enough that the underlying fact would be in any serious finance/tech curriculum or annual report from the last several years.
- **[RI] Reasonable inference** — follows from verified facts and common analytical frameworks.
- **[SO] Strategic opinion** — the author's allocator-style judgment; presented as opinion, not fact.
- **[NEV] Needs external validation** — directional claim that the reader (or a future research pass) should verify against current data before relying on it for capital decisions.

The book is written to the same discipline. Where the manuscript needs a number, it either uses an order-of-magnitude figure flagged as such, or it explains the *shape* of the number and tells the reader where to look it up.

## 1. Major economic forces behind the AI boom

- **[V]** A multi-decade reduction in the cost of compute, storage, and bandwidth created the substrate for modern deep learning.
- **[V]** Transformer architectures (introduced in academic literature in 2017) and the scaling-laws thesis demonstrated that sufficiently large models trained on sufficiently large corpora exhibit qualitatively new capabilities.
- **[V]** Capital expenditure by the largest cloud and consumer-internet platforms shifted heavily toward AI-accelerator infrastructure starting in the early 2020s.
- **[RI]** This is not a software product cycle. It is a **capital-allocation regime change**: a re-pricing of compute, electricity, data, talent, and distribution.
- **[RI]** Demand for AI compute is currently constrained by supply (advanced packaging, HBM memory, power, land). Constraints, not demand, set the pace.
- **[SO]** The boom most resembles the build-out of railroads, electrification, and the broadband internet — not the dot-com app layer. The infrastructure layer monetizes first; the application layer goes through a longer, messier shakeout.
- **[NEV]** Specific capex figures, energy demand projections, and chip-shipment numbers must be checked against current analyst reports.

## 2. The AI value chain

A practical decomposition the book uses end-to-end:

1. **Energy and land** — power generation, transmission, substation capacity, water for cooling, permitted sites.
2. **Semiconductors** — design (GPUs/TPUs/ASICs), advanced-node fabrication, packaging (e.g., advanced 2.5D/3D), high-bandwidth memory.
3. **Data-center hardware** — networking fabric, optical interconnect, cooling, racks, power distribution.
4. **Hyperscale cloud** — managed AI compute, storage, and inference platforms.
5. **Foundation models** — frontier and open-weight models, pre-training, post-training, alignment.
6. **Model serving / inference infrastructure** — orchestration, routing, caching, optimization.
7. **Data layer** — proprietary corpora, labeling, evaluation, synthetic data, vector databases.
8. **Tooling** — agent frameworks, RAG infrastructure, observability, guardrails, dev tools.
9. **Applications (horizontal)** — coding, productivity, search, customer support, sales, marketing.
10. **Applications (vertical)** — legal, medical, financial, industrial, defense, education.
11. **Robotics and embodied AI** — humanoid platforms, warehouse robotics, autonomous vehicles.
12. **Services** — consulting, integration, fine-tuning, custom deployment.
13. **Distribution** — devices, OSes, browsers, operating environments where AI is consumed.

**[SO]** Each layer has different gross margins, different capital intensity, different defensibility, and a different time-to-monetization. Treating "AI" as one investable theme is the single most common mistake non-professional investors make.

## 3. Ways individuals can monetize AI

**[V/RI] Established mechanisms:**

- **Wage leverage** — using AI tools to do existing knowledge work faster and at higher quality.
- **Skill arbitrage** — acquiring AI-adjacent skills (prompt engineering, evals, RAG, fine-tuning, agent design, AI product management) priced above their training cost.
- **Consulting and implementation** — billing for outcome-based AI projects in functions like sales, support, ops, finance, and marketing.
- **Productized services** — turning a service into a recurring offer with AI-driven delivery.
- **Micro-SaaS and indie products** — narrow tools that solve a specific workflow problem.
- **Content and audience building** — using AI to compress production cost while maintaining editorial quality.
- **Investing** — public equities, ETFs, and a small allocation to private vehicles where the investor has access and competence.
- **Career repositioning** — moving toward roles that capture AI-driven productivity gains rather than being absorbed by them.

**[SO]** The most underrated lever for almost everyone is **wage leverage inside an existing role** — it is lower-risk and faster-paying than entrepreneurship, and the compounding effect on career trajectory is large.

## 4. Ways businesses can monetize AI

- **Cost compression** — using AI to lower marginal cost in support, ops, content, code, finance, and back-office workflows.
- **Margin expansion** — using AI to deliver higher-value outputs at the same headcount.
- **Throughput scaling** — handling more volume per unit of fixed cost.
- **New product lines** — offering AI-native features customers will pay for.
- **Productizing internal tools** — turning a working internal AI workflow into an external product.
- **Roll-ups** — acquiring traditional service businesses and improving margins through AI-driven operating leverage.
- **Data monetization** — licensing or operationalizing proprietary data assets that AI systems need.
- **Vertical integration** — owning more of the customer workflow because AI lowers the cost of doing so.

**[SO]** The most durable business value generally accrues where AI is paired with proprietary data, a regulated workflow, a hard distribution moat, or an existing customer relationship — not where AI alone is the product.

## 5. How investors evaluate AI opportunities

A practical framework the book teaches in plain language:

1. **Where in the value chain?** — Different layers have very different economics.
2. **Capital intensity** — How much money must be spent to earn the next dollar of revenue?
3. **Defensibility** — What stops the next entrant from competing this away? (Distribution, data, regulation, switching cost, scale.)
4. **Unit economics** — Gross margin, contribution margin, payback period, retention.
5. **Customer concentration and counterparty risk** — Who are the buyers? How concentrated are they?
6. **Cycle position** — Is this a structurally early opportunity or a late-cycle re-rating?
7. **Valuation discipline** — Price paid governs return more than narrative.
8. **Personal edge** — What does the investor know or see that the marginal buyer does not?

## 6. Common scams, bubbles, and overhyped claims

**[V/RI] Recurring patterns:**

- "AI" tickers and shell companies pivoting in name only.
- Promotional newsletters and paid placements presented as research.
- "Guaranteed AI returns" trading systems and signal services.
- Pump-and-dump promotions on small-caps with thin AI narratives.
- AI-themed token offerings with weak or non-existent product utility.
- Course/coaching schemes promising six-figure incomes from "AI agencies" with no substance behind them.
- Fabricated case studies, AI-generated testimonials, and synthetic founder personas.
- Vendor demos that work in scripted conditions but fail in production.
- Capability inflation in pitch decks: confusing demoware with deployed product.
- Headcount theater: claiming AI displacement of jobs that were never real headcount.

**[SO]** The book treats these as a *taxonomy* — readers should be able to recognize each pattern when they see it, not just be warned in the abstract.

## 7. Historical parallels

- **Railroads (19th century)** — Heavy capex, infrastructure-first, multiple boom-bust cycles, eventual consolidation, durable operator value once shakeout completed.
- **Electrification** — Decades-long diffusion; productivity gains showed up only after process redesign.
- **Personal computer era** — Hardware margins compressed; software and platform economics dominated.
- **Internet (1995–2002)** — Infrastructure first (telecoms, routers, fiber), then a brutal application-layer shakeout, then a much larger durable wave (search, e-commerce, social).
- **Cloud (2006–present)** — Hyperscaler oligopoly with strong unit economics; SaaS layer eventually delivered very large compounders, with concentrated winners per category.
- **Mobile (2007–present)** — Devices, OSes, app stores, and distribution gates determined who captured the value.
- **Industrial automation** — Long, uneven adoption; payoffs concentrated in operators that re-engineered processes, not just bought equipment.

**[SO]** The clearest takeaway from these parallels: the **infrastructure layer compounds first and most reliably; the application layer produces a small number of very large winners and a long tail of failures.**

## 8. Key risks

- **Regulation** — labor, copyright, safety, export controls, sector-specific compliance (health, finance, defense).
- **Capital intensity** — frontier-model training costs are large and growing; financing structures matter.
- **Concentration risk** — a small number of customers, suppliers, and platforms dominate the stack.
- **Model commoditization** — capable open-weight models compress prices in many use cases.
- **Power and grid constraints** — siting, permitting, and energy availability are the new bottlenecks.
- **Job displacement and political backlash** — affects both narrative risk and policy risk.
- **Privacy, security, and data governance** — enterprise adoption gated on these.
- **Hallucination and reliability** — limits use in regulated and high-stakes workflows.
- **Cybersecurity** — AI attacks and AI-enabled fraud expand the attack surface.
- **Asset-price bubbles** — narrative-driven rerating ahead of cash flows; risk of multi-year drawdowns even in eventual winners.
- **Geopolitical risk** — chip supply chain, export controls, foreign-policy shocks.
- **Personal risk** — concentrated bets, leverage, illiquid private exposure, scams.

## 9. Editorial source-discipline rules (apply to every chapter)

1. No invented statistics. If a number is needed and cannot be verified from memory of widely-reported, durable data, the manuscript uses an *order-of-magnitude* phrasing or describes the *shape* of the number.
2. No invented quotes from real people. Period.
3. No invented case studies presented as real. Composite scenarios are clearly framed as illustrative.
4. No invented company financial results.
5. No invented laws, rulings, or regulatory text.
6. Where a current figure or ruling matters, the manuscript tells the reader to verify it against a current source.
7. The book contains no personalized financial advice; it teaches frameworks and is explicit about that boundary.
